### Common model conventions

- Revenue is built from operating units (firms, accounts, organizations, factories) — not a top-down market-growth percentage. Forecasts represent an HCP base case and are not management guidance.
- Entry ownership equals check divided by post-money valuation. Where a post-money valuation is publicly disclosed (Basis, Spellbook, Squint, Eve) the memo uses that figure and says so; otherwise the post-money is an explicit **HCP assumption** built from round size and typical dilution.
- Exit ownership equals entry ownership multiplied by one minus cumulative dilution. Dilution is set at 25-40% depending on how many priced rounds HCP expects between entry and a 2032 exit: 25% for the two already-unicorn Series B names (Basis, Eve, fewer rounds likely before exit), 30% for the other five Series B names, 35% for Artisan (mature Series A), and 40% for the two youngest primings (Kick, Concourse).
- Exit enterprise value equals 2032 revenue multiplied by the selected EV/LTM revenue multiple. HCP proceeds equal exit enterprise value multiplied by exit ownership. MOIC equals proceeds divided by check. The model excludes preference effects, taxes, fees, secondary sales, and follow-on reserves.
- Public-comps multiples are snapshots as of July 18, 2026 from the linked StockAnalysis pages. Five multiples (MongoDB, Snowflake, Cloudflare, Datadog, GitLab) are reused verbatim from the reference file's Mem0 memo, which sourced them from StockAnalysis on the same date; all other tickers in this batch were fetched fresh from stockanalysis.com/stocks/TICKER/statistics/ on July 18, 2026.
- Downside uses 60% of base exit revenue and one turn less on the multiple, with a 1.0x floor. Upside uses 150% of base exit revenue and one turn more. Dilution is held constant.
- Competitive tables use **X** only where the capability is verified, **Partial** where the product covers only part of the workflow, **No evidence** where public material does not support the claim, and **Undisclosed** for unavailable commercial or capital data.

### Product description

Concourse is an AI agent platform built for corporate finance teams. It connects to ERPs, data warehouses, billing systems, payroll, and CRMs, then lets finance staff query data, generate CFO-ready reports on a schedule, and export results to PDF, Excel, or PowerPoint with every number traceable back to source data. Concourse moved from stealth to general availability in January 2026 alongside its Series A, with a free-to-start onboarding flow layered under paid enterprise tiers.

### Thesis: why invest

Concourse announced a $12m Series A on January 27, 2026, led by Standard Capital with Andreessen Horowitz, CRV, Y Combinator, SV Angel, FOG Ventures, and Ritual Capital participating, alongside CFO angels from Cursor, Vercel, and Carta. **What must be true:** the reported 19x growth must be durable and not a stealth-to-GA base-rate artifact; auditability must hold up under real audit and SOX scrutiny at larger accounts; and the platform must expand from analysis and reporting into higher-stakes financial actions without eroding trust.

**Next-round milestones:** $15m ARR, 500+ paying finance teams, net revenue retention above 130%, at least ten Fortune 500 or public-company references, and evidence that growth is decelerating gracefully rather than falling off a stealth-launch cliff.

### Product description

Dust is a "multiplayer" agentic AI platform for the enterprise: rather than giving each employee an isolated personal assistant, Dust lets humans and AI agents share workspaces, context, and workflows across an entire company. Assistants can be connected to Notion, Google Drive, Slack, Intercom, and other internal systems, and the platform is built around collaborative, auditable agent use rather than single-player chat. As of the May 2026 Series B, Dust reported more than 3,000 organizations on the platform, 51,000 monthly active users, zero customer churn in 2025, and 300,000 agents deployed.

### Thesis: why invest

Dust closed a $40m Series B on May 18, 2026, led by Abstract and Sequoia with Snowflake Ventures and Datadog participating, following a $16m Series A led by Sequoia in mid-2024 (with XYZ, GG1, Connect Ventures, Seedcamp, and Motier Ventures). Total disclosed funding is over $60m. Zero churn across all of 2025 is an unusually strong retention signal for enterprise software at this stage, and strategic investment from Snowflake and Datadog suggests real technical and go-to-market interest from adjacent data-infrastructure players.

The moat is workspace-level context and multiplayer workflow design: once a company's agents, data connections, and collaborative processes live inside Dust, replacing it means rebuilding an operational layer, not just swapping a chatbot. If enterprise AI shifts from single-user assistants toward shared, auditable agent operations — which the zero-churn and 300,000-agent figures suggest is already happening among Dust's base — Dust's positioning compounds with every new team and workflow added.

**What must be true:** the reported zero-2025-churn must persist as the customer base scales past early adopters; usage (MAU, agents deployed) must convert into expanding paid seats and modules, not just engagement; and Dust must hold ground against Microsoft Copilot's default distribution and Glean's enterprise-search-first wedge.

**Next-round milestones:** 10,000+ organizations, $30m+ ARR, net revenue retention above 120%, gross retention above 95%, and at least five reference accounts with more than 1,000 seats.

### Founder bet

The bet is that a research-and-engineering-led team based in Paris can win a category Microsoft, Google, and well-funded U.S. competitors are also chasing, by being first to a genuinely collaborative, multi-agent workflow model rather than a single-assistant product. Diligence should probe technical differentiation versus Glean and Copilot specifically, and whether the France/Europe home base is an advantage (data residency, EU enterprise trust) or a distribution constraint versus U.S.-based competitors.

### Product description

Squint is a mobile, AI- and AR-based platform that captures expert knowledge from experienced factory operators and turns it into step-by-step digital procedures. ### Thesis: why invest

## Investment committee conclusion

HCP should prioritize Concourse, Dust, and Squint for direct diligence: each combines real disclosed operating traction (19x/13x growth, zero 2025 churn, marquee Fortune 500 logos, respectively) with an entry price small enough for a $1.0m-$2.5m check to buy a return-relevant ownership stake. Kick and Artisan merit diligence at a lower conviction level — both have genuine usage evidence but sit in structurally crowded, margin-pressured categories (SMB bookkeeping's Bench precedent; AI SDR's intense competitive intensity) that cap the base case below a strong Pursue. Numeric, HappyRobot, and Spellbook are Price-sensitive: all three are good businesses with real enterprise or professional-services traction, but each has already priced a Series B (disclosed or estimated) large enough that HCP's assumed or confirmed entry valuation compresses the achievable MOIC into the 1.4x-1.9x range: pursue only if the actual valuation comes in below HCP's assumption, or with a smaller check. Basis and Eve are Pass at today's price: both are excellent operating businesses (30% of the Top 25 U.S. accounting firms; 500% YoY revenue growth at Series A) that simply moved to unicorn-scale pricing faster than a small check can profitably follow — track both for a later-stage vehicle or a structured secondary rather than a primary check now.

No memo should advance on public evidence alone. The next step for the Pursue and Diligence names is a standardized data request covering cap table and terms, monthly revenue and gross margin, customer or account cohorts, retention, security and compliance posture, and reference calls — with particular attention to confirming actual round valuations for Numeric, HappyRobot, Dust, Kick, Artisan, and Concourse, none of which publicly disclosed a precise post-money figure in sources reviewed.
