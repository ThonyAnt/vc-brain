### Common model conventions

- Revenue is built from operating units (paid subscribers, GMV and take rate, delivery orders, renters, matched participants), not a top-down market-growth percentage. Nearly every company in this batch discloses little or no revenue publicly; forecasts represent an HCP base case and are explicitly not management guidance.
- Entry ownership equals check divided by post-money valuation. Exit ownership equals entry ownership multiplied by one minus cumulative dilution. Dilution is 35% (HCP assumption) except Chowdeck, where HCP uses 40% to reflect the additional primary capital a physical-logistics network in emerging markets is likely to require before exit.
- Exit enterprise value equals 2032 revenue multiplied by the selected EV/LTM revenue multiple. HCP proceeds equal exit enterprise value multiplied by exit ownership. MOIC equals proceeds divided by check. The model excludes preference effects, taxes, fees, secondary sales, and follow-on reserves.
- Public-comps multiples are snapshots as of July 18, 2026 from the linked StockAnalysis pages, whose financial statistics cite S&P Global Market Intelligence. Several comps in this batch (Bumble, Snap, Eventbrite, Vivid Seats) trade below 1.5x EV/LTM revenue, which mechanically caps base-case MOICs for consumer-social and events companies unless a memo argues for a premium and explains why.
- Downside uses 60% of base exit revenue and one turn less on the multiple, with a 1.0x floor. Upside uses 150% of base exit revenue and one turn more. Dilution is held constant. Where the base multiple is already at or near 1.0x, the floor means the downside multiple equals the base multiple and only the revenue haircut drives the downside MOIC — this occurs for several companies in this batch and is a deliberate, not erroneous, consequence of thin comp multiples.
- Competitive tables use **X** only where the capability is verified, **Partial** where the product covers only part of the workflow, **No evidence** where public material does not support the claim, and **Undisclosed** for unavailable commercial or capital data.

### Product description

Chowdeck is an on-demand delivery platform serving Nigeria and Ghana. It started with restaurant food delivery and has expanded into groceries, pharmacy/healthcare items, a vendor marketplace (Chowstore), an advertising product, a subscription tier (Chowpass), and a package-relay service. The company operates its own rider network — more than 20,000 riders as of the August 2025 Series A report — and emphasizes density-driven efficiency: average delivery time is about 30 minutes, and in dense urban zones more than half of deliveries are completed by bicycle. Unlike most venture-funded delivery startups, Chowdeck says it has stayed profitable while scaling, a rare claim in a notoriously low-margin category, and TechCrunch reports the company is now building out quick-commerce dark stores to capture higher-frequency, higher-margin grocery demand.

### Thesis: why invest

Chowdeck raised a $9m Series A in August 2025 led by Novastar Ventures, with Y Combinator, AAIC Investment, Rebel Fund, GFR Fund, Kaleo, and HoaQ participating, per TechCrunch. The company reports 1.5 million customers across 11 cities in Nigeria and Ghana, meal-delivery value that grew more than sixfold in 2024, and 2025 volume that surpassed all of 2024 before July. These are company-reported operating metrics, not audited figures, but they are specific and directionally consistent across two independent outlets (TechCrunch, AfroTech).

The moat is operational, not algorithmic: a dense, well-utilized rider network in cities where organized last-mile delivery infrastructure barely existed a few years ago. Chowdeck's claimed profitability while scaling — a genuinely rare trait in food delivery anywhere — suggests real unit-economics discipline rather than growth bought with unsustainable subsidies. The multi-vertical expansion into grocery, pharmacy, and a merchant marketplace gives Chowdeck several ways to raise basket size and frequency per rider-hour, which is the core lever in delivery economics.

**What must be true:** quick-commerce dark-store expansion (40 stores by end-2025, 500 targeted by end-2026 per the company) must reach positive contribution margin quickly; profitability must hold as Chowdeck adds lower-density cities and new verticals; and currency and macro conditions in Nigeria must not erode unit economics faster than volume growth compounds them.

**Next-round milestones:** $250m annualized delivery+quick-commerce GMV, sustained consolidated profitability (not just per-city), successful unit economics at 100+ dark stores, expansion into at least one additional West African market, and rider-network retention/utilization data supporting further densification.

### Founder bet

Aluko, Ojo, and Yusuf built Chowdeck through Y Combinator and have executed a genuinely difficult operating playbook — dense rider logistics in infrastructure-constrained cities — while reportedly staying profitable, which most global delivery platforms (including well-capitalized ones) have struggled to do. The bet is that this founding team's operating discipline, demonstrated over roughly four years and multiple funding rounds, extends cleanly into quick commerce and a second country. Diligence should verify the profitability claim at the consolidated (not per-city) level and confirm rider classification/compliance posture ahead of scale.

### Product description

Bezel is a technology-enabled marketplace for buying, selling, and trading pre-owned luxury watches. Every watch is shipped to Bezel's in-house team before reaching the buyer, where specialists inspect movement, case, and provenance and issue a digital and physical certificate of authenticity. The platform lists thousands of watches (the company's own site says listings represent over $500m in inventory value) spanning brands such as Rolex, Patek Philippe, and Audemars Piguet, and offers wishlist/price-alert tools, market-intelligence/price-tracking features built from transaction data, and a concierge-sourcing service. Average order value is reported above $10,000. The wedge is trust: peer-to-peer and dealer watch trading has long suffered from counterfeit and provenance risk, and Bezel centralizes authentication rather than relying on buyer or seller self-certification.

### Thesis: why invest

Bezel's own August-2023-dated post describes an $8m seed round (closed around January 2023) with BoxGroup, Courtside VC, Shrug Capital, Abstract Ventures, Operator Partners, and others, plus a notable roster of celebrity investors (Kevin Hart's HartBeat Ventures, John Legend, Steve Aoki, J Balvin). A separate October 2024 Forbes profile refers to the same or an earlier round as a "Series A raised in August 2021" led by BoxGroup and states the company was "currently bootstrapping without active fundraising" as of that date. These sources conflict on both the round's name and timing, and aggregator totals for lifetime funding range from roughly $10m to $15.6m — HCP was unable to find a single authoritative financing figure and flags this explicitly for diligence. What is consistent across sources: Bezel has raised a real, credible seed-stage round from institutional and strategic investors, has meaningful listed inventory value, and reports it has not needed to raise again as of late 2024, which is either a sign of capital efficiency or a signal the company should be pressed on in diligence.

The category is attractive because trust, not supply, is the bottleneck in watch resale — Bezel's authentication-first model directly targets that bottleneck, and celebrity/collector investor participation suggests real community credibility in a category where taste and reputation matter.

**What must be true:** authentication throughput must scale without becoming a cost or speed bottleneck; average order value and repeat-purchase rate must support marketing spend given "lowest CAC in the industry" claims; and Bezel must maintain trust as inventory volume grows past what a small in-house team can personally vet.

**Next-round milestones:** disclosed GMV run-rate above $150m, take-rate/revenue transparency sufficient to model unit economics, evidence of repeat buyers and sellers (not just one-time transactions), and a clear answer on whether/when the company intends to raise a priced Series A.

### Founder bet

Walker's own frustration as a Rolex buyer is a classic founder-market-fit story, and a product-and-design background at Google is a reasonable asset for building trustworthy e-commerce UX in a category where presentation and confidence matter. The founding team composition beyond Walker is the least well-documented part of this file (see the caveat on Chase Payan/Pion and Darryl Johnson above) and should be confirmed directly in diligence before any capital commitment.

### Product description

Timeleft matches small groups of strangers — typically five to six people — for a recurring weekly dinner, based on a compatibility questionnaire. Events run every Wednesday (with additional formats added over time) in restaurants Timeleft partners with, explicitly positioned as neither a dating app nor a networking app but a tool for building real-world friendship. The service has scaled from a single-city experiment (the first event drew 25 diners) to more than 200 cities across 52 countries by December 2025, per the company's own site, with recent expansions into London and Liverpool. The company reports meaningful, disclosed recurring revenue (see below), which distinguishes it from most other companies in this batch.

### Thesis: why invest

Timeleft raised a $7m Series A in July 2024, with Redstone, EnjoyVenture, Smash Capital, FJ Labs, and Global Founders Capital among its investors, per aggregator and funding-database reporting (HCP could not locate a primary press release for this specific round and flags it for diligence confirmation). Independent reporting (via a founder-profile piece and the company's own posts) states Timeleft reached roughly €18m in annualized recurring revenue and 150,000 monthly dinner participants across 200+ cities by August 2025 — real, disclosed operating scale that is unusual for this batch, where most companies have no public revenue at all.

The loneliness and adult-friendship problem Timeleft addresses is real and widely documented, and the company has found a repeatable, low-cost-to-replicate-per-city format (a recurring Wednesday ritual, matched by algorithm, hosted at partner restaurants) that has scaled to more cities and countries than most consumer social startups reach. The moat is the compatibility-matching data and city-by-city operating playbook (restaurant partnerships, local marketing, safety protocols) built over roughly 200 city launches — a genuine execution asset that is hard to replicate quickly.

**What must be true:** growth must continue converting into paying, repeat participants rather than one-time novelty-seekers; the restaurant-partner network must scale without service-quality dilution; and the recurring-dinner format must retain differentiation as competitors (Pie, 222, and others in this batch) enter the same broad "IRL loneliness" category.

**Next-round milestones:** $30m+ annualized revenue in USD terms, disclosed repeat-participation and retention rates, profitability or a clear path to it at the city level, and continued expansion into major markets (Asia-Pacific, per the company's stated goals) without diluting per-city unit economics.

### Founder bet

Barbier's path — nightlife promotion, then media, then Timeleft — reads as a founder who repeatedly tests ideas against real audience response rather than a single long-held thesis; the Temy profile frames the first Timeleft dinner (25 diners) growing through successive events (35, 70, 81 participants) as exactly that kind of iterative, feedback-driven build. That pattern is a plausible explanation for how a small founding team reached 200+ cities faster than better-funded competitors in this batch. Diligence should confirm de Oliveira's current operating scope and assess how much of the city-expansion playbook is genuinely repeatable versus founder-dependent.

## Investment committee conclusion

HCP should prioritize Chowdeck for direct diligence: it is the only company in this batch with disclosed, multi-source-corroborated revenue scale, a genuine and rare profitability claim in a hard operating category, and a base MOIC that clears this program's return bar with meaningful margin. Bezel, Timeleft, Beli, and Pickle merit real diligence work — each has a credible product and at least partial disclosed evidence, though the specific gaps differ (Bezel's financing-history opacity, Beli's total absence of monetization evidence, Pickle's need for lender-quality data, and Timeleft's FX and category-crowding exposure). Partiful, 222, and Yope are Watch-list companies with real strengths (Partiful's cultural relevance, 222's investor syndicate, Yope's exceptional retention) that are each blocked by the same core gap: no disclosed monetization evidence anywhere HCP could find. Posh and Pie are Pass recommendations for different reasons — Posh on price (a fully disclosed $253m valuation that this program's check size cannot underwrite to an attractive return), and Pie on business-model structure (a cost-negative core loop with zero offsetting revenue evidence and the slowest geographic expansion of its direct peer set).

The next step for any Watch or Diligence company is a standardized data request covering cap table and terms, monthly revenue (if any) and gross margin, user/customer cohorts, acquisition channels and cost, retention, and pipeline. Given how many companies in this batch have disclosed no revenue at all, the single highest-value diligence question across the batch is simply: does a monetization product exist, even in test form, and what did it show?
