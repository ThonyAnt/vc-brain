### Common model conventions

- Revenue is built from operating units (paid seats, accounts, districts, creators, or teachers), not a top-down market-growth percentage. Forecasts represent an HCP base case and are not management guidance.
- Entry ownership equals check divided by post-money valuation. Exit ownership equals entry ownership multiplied by one minus cumulative dilution. Dilution is 35%, the batch standard, except Fyxer (25%, explained in its memo given its later stage).
- Exit enterprise value equals 2032 revenue multiplied by the selected EV/LTM revenue multiple. HCP proceeds equal exit enterprise value multiplied by exit ownership. MOIC equals proceeds divided by check. The model excludes preference effects, taxes, fees, secondary sales, and follow-on reserves.
- Public-comps multiples are snapshots as of July 18, 2026 from the linked StockAnalysis pages. Multiples already cited for a given ticker in the reference file (`HCP_10_Investment_Memos_2026-07-18.md`) are reused rather than re-fetched; new tickers were fetched fresh from stockanalysis.com for this batch.
- Downside uses 60% of base exit revenue and one turn less on the multiple, with a 1.0x floor. Upside uses 150% of base exit revenue and one turn more. Dilution is held constant.
- Competitive tables use **X** only where the capability is verified, **Partial** where the product covers only part of the workflow, **No evidence** where public material does not support the claim, and **Undisclosed** for unavailable commercial or capital data.
- Two education- and creator-software public comps that would otherwise be relevant — PowerSchool and Instructure — were taken private (Bain Capital, 2024, and KKR, 2024, respectively) and are therefore unavailable as public comps; this is noted where relevant.

### Product description

Howie is an AI assistant that lives inside email, acting as a virtual scheduling secretary. A user CCs Howie on a thread and the system negotiates times across participants, adds events to calendars, follows up automatically, and audits a user's schedule for conflicts, with a human-in-the-loop layer correcting mistakes before they reach the customer. The wedge is narrow and specific — scheduling, not general inbox management — which lets Howie compete with the manual-labor-heavy world of executive assistants and freelance schedulers rather than broad AI-email suites. GeekWire reported more than 1,000 paying customers and 5,000-plus meetings scheduled weekly at public launch in September 2025, with monthly recurring revenue and usage growing an average of 50% month over month through 2025 (company-reported figures).

### Thesis: why invest

Howie raised a $6m seed round in September 2025 led by Sequoia and Andreessen Horowitz at a disclosed $30m valuation, with participation from Sophia Amoruso (Trust Fund), Turner Novak (Banana Capital), Gaby Goldberg (Rostra), Lindel Eakman (Foundry Group), Sheel Mohnot, Resolute Ventures, PJC, and Roo Capital — an unusually strong seed syndicate for a narrow-scope product. The disclosed $30m post-money valuation against more than 1,000 paying customers and reported 50% month-over-month growth is inexpensive relative to the batch's other companies, most of which carry HCP-assumed (undisclosed) valuations.

The narrowness of the product is the moat candidate: a single-purpose scheduling agent can be more reliable than a general assistant bolt-on, and the human-in-the-loop layer buys trust while the underlying model handles more of the negotiation over time. If Howie becomes the default way professionals hand off scheduling friction, it can expand into adjacent coordination tasks (travel, intros, follow-ups) from a beachhead of proven reliability.

**What must be true:** the 50% month-over-month growth rate must decelerate gracefully into a durable expansion curve rather than stall; the human-in-the-loop cost structure must scale without eroding gross margin; and Howie must win against free scheduling features bundled into Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, and Calendly before those vendors close the reliability gap.

**Next-round milestones:** 10,000 paying customers, $10m ARR, gross margin above 70% after human-review costs, sub-24-hour scheduling resolution on 95% of requests, and net revenue retention above 115%.

### Founder bet

Petersmith and Newman have worked together since 2013 and tested this exact idea in 2015, before abandoning it when the technology wasn't ready — a decade-long conviction that is a genuine signal, not a pivot of convenience. The bet is that operator patience plus a narrow, trust-building product surface beats a broad, feature-heavy AI assistant race. Diligence should verify the current team's ability to hire and retain the human reviewers the model depends on, and confirm unit economics per scheduled meeting.

### Principal risks and why invest anyway

### Product description

Chronicle is an AI presentation maker that converts documents, notes, data, and business context into editable, branded slide decks on a free-form canvas rather than a rigid template grid. Distinguishing features include "Peek" and "Deep Hover," which let presenters zoom into or isolate specific content live, and AI-driven research, narrative structuring, and design execution intended to remove the blank-page problem. Output exports to PowerPoint, PDF, or a web-hosted format. Chronicle publicly launched in June 2025 after building a 100,000-person waitlist entirely through organic word-of-mouth, and the company's own site claims usage by more than 5,000 teams, including named accounts at Ramp, Accenture, Adobe, Figma, and others.

### Thesis: why invest

Chronicle raised $7.5m in a 2023 seed round from Accel, Square Peg, and angels from Apple, Google, Meta, Slack, Stripe, and Adobe — a strong signal syndicate for a company that had not yet publicly launched. The subsequent 100,000-user waitlist and 10,000-business interest list, generated with "zero marketing" per the founders, is an unusually strong organic-demand signal for a presentation tool in a category most people assume is commoditized. No Series A has been publicly confirmed as of July 2026, three years after the seed — HCP treats the proposed check as a bridge/extension-stage investment into a company whose commercial traction may have outpaced its last priced round.

The wedge is narrative and design quality rather than raw generation speed: Chronicle is positioned against both legacy tools (PowerPoint, Google Slides) and newer AI-generation competitors (Gamma, Canva, Beautiful.ai) on the basis of editable, professional-grade output rather than one-shot generation. If Chronicle becomes the default way operators build investor and customer-facing narratives, the company inherits a durable, high-frequency workflow.

**What must be true:** the 100,000-person waitlist must convert into paying seats at a rate that funds the business; design quality must remain differentiated as native AI features arrive inside PowerPoint and Google Slides; and the freemium funnel must not cannibalize the enterprise upsell.

**Next-round milestones:** $10m ARR, 1,000 paying business accounts, net revenue retention above 115%, and at least three enterprise logos converted from the named-account waitlist into contracted, multi-seat deals.

### Founder bet

Patole's decade of hands-on presentation-consulting experience is a credible source of product taste in a category where most competitors are led by generalist AI-tools founders; Gawande's growth background plausibly explains the zero-spend, organic 100,000-user waitlist. Diligence should test whether the founding team can build enterprise sales motion on top of a bottoms-up, virality-driven growth engine, since those two muscles are rarely built by the same team.

### Product description

Fyxer is an AI executive-assistant product that organizes inboxes, drafts email replies, and produces meeting notes and summaries. Its differentiation claim rests on data lineage: the founders built and operated a real executive-assistant agency before building the software, and say they used roughly 500,000 hours of that operational data to train the product's understanding of how EA workflows actually run. The product integrates with calendar and inbox systems and adds HubSpot CRM integration, multi-inbox support, and a "Fyxer Chat" instant-answer feature at higher tiers. Pricing runs from $22.50 to $50 per user per month depending on tier and billing cadence, with custom enterprise pricing above 50 seats.

### Thesis: why invest

Fyxer's growth trajectory is the standout fact in this batch: Madrona and multiple outlets report ARR growing from about $1m at the start of 2025 to $17m by August 2025, and Forbes reported a further jump to $25m in revenue by November 2025 — an extremely fast ramp for a horizontal productivity product. The company disclosed more than 180,000 users (Sifted) and 90% three-month retention at the time of its Series B. The Series B was led by Madrona (known for backing UiPath) with Lakestar participating, on top of the March 2025 Series A led by 20VC with Marc Benioff and other angels; total disclosed funding is $43m, though the Series B valuation itself was not disclosed.

The founders' data-lineage story — training on real EA workflow data rather than generic email corpora — is the most plausible durable advantage in a category (AI email/meeting assistants) that is otherwise easy to enter. Whether that data edge compounds as competitors accumulate their own usage data, or erodes as foundation models improve generally, is the central diligence question.

**What must be true:** the ARR growth rate must not have been a one-time launch spike; retention must hold as the user base moves from early adopters to mainstream professionals; and the EA-workflow data advantage must remain defensible as Superhuman, Read AI, and native Gmail/Outlook Copilot features close the capability gap.

**Next-round milestones:** $50m ARR (management's own stated year-end target per press coverage), net revenue retention above 120%, gross margin above 75% after LLM inference costs, and a validated enterprise (50+ seat) segment growing faster than SMB.

### Founder bet

The Hollingsworth brothers and Ffrench are a rare combination: operators who ran the actual labor-intensive service before automating it, rather than engineers guessing at a workflow from the outside. That lineage explains both the unusually fast initial product-market fit and the plausibility of the proprietary-data claim. Diligence should verify how much of the original 500,000-hour dataset remains a meaningful training advantage today versus general-purpose model improvements, and assess founder bandwidth as the team doubles headcount within six months of the Series B.

## Investment committee conclusion

HCP should prioritize Howie for direct diligence: it combines the batch's only disclosed, inexpensive entry valuation with strong disclosed growth and an unusually experienced founding pair. Chronicle, Fyxer, Napkin, and Happenstance merit diligence in that order — each has a real product signal, but each also carries a specific, resolvable information gap (stale financing for Chronicle and Napkin, undisclosed Series B pricing for Fyxer, disputed funding figures for Happenstance) that should be closed before a check is wired. Delphi is a genuine, differentiated category bet backed by a strong syndicate, but its return math depends on broadening revenue beyond a handful of outlier creators.

SchoolAI and Brisk Teaching both show the batch's most impressive usage scale — SchoolAI in particular, at 1 million classrooms and ESSA Level III validation — but the public education-software comp set (weighed down by Chegg's AI-disruption discount) caps the modeled return at the assumed entry prices. Both are Price-sensitive: HCP should pursue them only at a lower entry valuation or with structural downside protection, not at the terms implied by their most recent disclosed rounds.

Lindy (this batch's roster-swap replacement for the now-acquired Class Companion) shows a real product signal but has the thinnest, most contradictory public financing record in the batch; it is a Watch pending a direct company conversation, not a Diligence. Ello has the strongest founding team on paper but the weakest evidence of continued momentum and the weakest modeled return, with a downside case that loses money outright; HCP recommends passing unless the company can demonstrate confirmed recent growth.

No memo should advance on public evidence alone. The next step for every Diligence-or-better company is a standardized data request covering cap table and terms, monthly revenue and gross margin, customer or user cohorts, retention, and reference calls — with particular urgency on Fyxer's actual Series B valuation, Happenstance's and Lindy's true capitalization, and SchoolAI's and Brisk Teaching's paid-conversion metrics.
